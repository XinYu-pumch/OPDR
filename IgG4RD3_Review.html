1 Introduction

1.1 IgG4-Related Disease (IgG4-RD) is a recently recognized systemic fibro-inflammatory condition.

IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is a recently described systemic, immune-mediated fibro-inflammatory condition that can affect nearly any organ system (PMID: 25415530, 28374231). Originally proposed in Japan, the current concept of IgG4-RD encompasses several previously unrelated single-organ diseases that share distinct clinical, serological, and histopathological features (PMID: 32279291, 22487688). The disease is characteristically associated with tumefactive lesions, elevated serum IgG4 concentrations, and specific tissue findings, including abundant IgG4-positive plasma cell infiltration and storiform fibrosis (PMID: 33681509, 35221040). Most patients exhibit a favorable initial response to corticosteroid therapy, which remains a hallmark of the clinical presentation (PMID: 33681509, 35221040).

1.2 IgG4-Related Respiratory Disease (IgG4-RRD) represents a significant and common manifestation of systemic IgG4-RD.

IgG4-related respiratory disease (IgG4-RRD) is a major component of the systemic disease spectrum, characterized by a variety of intrathoracic manifestations (PMID: 25422997, 40615976). Clinical symptoms are often nonspecific, including cough, dyspnea, and chest pain, which can lead to diagnostic challenges (PMID: 25422997). IgG4-RRD frequently mimics more common pulmonary conditions such as lung cancer, sarcoidosis, interstitial lung disease, and pneumonia (PMID: 40615976, 37632955). Furthermore, it may present as atypical or treatment-resistant asthma or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), necessitating a high index of suspicion for accurate diagnosis (PMID: 40615976).

1.3 The epidemiology of IgG4-RRD shows a predominance in middle-aged to elderly men.

Epidemiological studies indicate that IgG4-RD predominantly affects middle-aged to elderly individuals, with a median age at diagnosis typically between 55 and 65 years (PMID: 26481243, 30474465, 39644037). There is a striking male preponderance, with men accounting for approximately 60% to 87% of cases, although this gender bias is less pronounced in patients with head and neck involvement (PMID: 30135634, 24107849, 39644037). In large cohorts of patients with systemic IgG4-RD, the prevalence of lung involvement has been reported to range from 17.6% to 23.4%, highlighting its significance in clinical practice (PMID: 30474465).

1.4 This review aims to synthesize recent advances in IgG4-RRD, from pathogenic mechanisms to clinical practice.

As awareness of this condition grows, our understanding of its complex etiology and clinical phenotypes continues to expand (PMID: 24296677, 40195520). This review aims to provide a comprehensive synthesis of recent developments in IgG4-RRD, focusing on the underlying pathogenic mechanisms involving genetic and environmental drivers (PMID: 40195520, 32279291). Furthermore, we will discuss advances in diagnostic approaches and the evolution of therapeutic strategies, including the transition from standard glucocorticoids to emerging B-cell depleting agents and other targeted biological therapies (PMID: 33689549, 40195520).

2 Pathogenesis and Immunology

2.1 The adaptive immune system plays a central role in the pathogenesis of IgG4-RRD.

The pathogenesis of IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD), including its respiratory manifestations (IgG4-RRD), is not fully understood, but there is a strong consensus that the adaptive immune system is a key driver of the disease process (PMID: 34539675, 27744509). The disease is characterized by a complex interplay between various immune cells, particularly T and B lymphocytes, which orchestrate the fibro-inflammatory response that ultimately leads to tissue damage and organ dysfunction (PMID: 33689549, 40398200). While the initial trigger remains unknown, the immune response shows features of being antigen-driven, as suggested by the oligoclonal expansion of lymphocyte populations in affected tissues (PMID: 33689549).

2.1.1 Dysregulation of T-cell subsets, including T helper 2, regulatory T, and cytotoxic T cells, drives the fibro-inflammatory process.

A dysregulated T-cell response is a cornerstone of IgG4-RD pathogenesis. A distinctive feature is the predominance of a T helper 2 (Th2) cell response and the activation of regulatory T (Treg) cells in affected sites (PMID: 40809241, 24107849). This upregulation leads to the secretion of a specific cytokine profile, including interleukin (IL)-4, IL-5, IL-10, IL-13, and transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) (PMID: 27012661, 24107849). This cytokine milieu is believed to promote key pathological processes, including the activation of B-cells, class-switching to IgG4, and the development of fibrosis (PMID: 27012661, 40342085).

More recently, other T-cell subsets have been identified as crucial players. CD4+ T follicular helper (Tfh) cells, especially the Tfh2 subset, are considered central in stimulating B-cell proliferation and driving the IgG4 isotype class switch (PMID: 33689549, 40342085). The number of circulating Tfh2 cells has been shown to correlate with disease activity and serum IgG4 levels, and it decreases after glucocorticoid therapy (PMID: 40342085). Furthermore, cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs), including CD4+SLAMF7+ CTLs and T peripheral helper (Tph) cells, are implicated in causing direct tissue damage through the release of cytotoxic molecules like granzyme and perforin, as well as contributing to the pro-fibrotic environment (PMID: 33689549, 40342085).

2.1.2 The activation and expansion of B-cell lineages, including plasmablasts and IgG4-producing plasma cells, are hallmark features of the disease.

The B-cell lineage plays a central role in the immunopathology of IgG4-RD (PMID: 40342085, 38601138). This is supported by several key observations: the histopathological hallmark of a dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate rich in IgG4-positive plasma cells, elevated serum IgG4 concentrations, and the presence of oligoclonally expanded plasmablasts in the circulation that decrease upon remission (PMID: 38601138, 27744509). The clinical success of B-cell depletion therapy with agents like rituximab further solidifies the pathogenic importance of B-cells (PMID: 27744509). The activation of B-cells is driven by cytokines secreted from the dysregulated T-cell populations (PMID: 27012661). Studies of the B-cell receptor repertoire have revealed oligoclonal expansions and signs of affinity maturation, suggesting an ongoing, antigen-driven response (PMID: 40342085). Interestingly, the plasmablast clones that reappear during a disease relapse are often distinct from those present during the initial active phase, indicating that new naïve B-cells are continuously recruited into the disease process (PMID: 40342085).

2.1.3 The precise pathogenic role of the IgG4 molecule itself, whether causative or a bystander, remains a subject of investigation.

Despite being the namesake of the disease, the exact role of the IgG4 molecule in pathogenesis is obscure and a topic of significant debate (PMID: 30135634, 32279291). It remains uncertain whether the elevated levels of IgG4 are a primary driver of the fibro-inflammatory process or merely a secondary consequence or biomarker of an underlying immune dysregulation (PMID: 40809241, 26259069). One prominent hypothesis suggests that IgG4 may not be pathogenic but rather acts as an anti-inflammatory or regulatory antibody. The natural properties of IgG4, such as its inability to activate the complement cascade or form stable immune complexes, support the idea that it may serve to dampen inflammatory or allergic reactions (PMID: 30135634, 27012661). From this perspective, the surge in IgG4 could be a counter-regulatory response to an unknown trigger, making it an "active witness" rather than the primary culprit (PMID: 30135634). However, a direct pathogenic role has not been ruled out, and further investigation is required to elucidate whether IgG4 is a pathogenic effector, a protective bystander, or both (PMID: 22487688, 24107849).

2.2 The innate immune system is implicated in the initiation and perpetuation of the disease.

The pathogenesis of IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is thought to begin with the activation of the innate immune system, which serves as a prerequisite for the subsequent adaptive immune response and autoantibody production (PMID: 40342085). A plausible model suggests that in genetically susceptible individuals, an environmental trigger, such as a microbial encounter, initiates tissue damage and a breakdown in immunological tolerance (PMID: 25481618, 37809072). This initial insult is believed to trigger an innate immune response that, through extensive crosstalk with the adaptive immune system, drives the disease process (PMID: 40342085). Although the immunology of IgG4-RD points toward a predominantly T-helper 2 (Th2) cell-mediated response, the precise inciting mechanisms and the initial steps involving the innate immune system remain poorly understood and are an active area of investigation (PMID: 32279291, 30135634).

2.3 The immune response in IgG4-RD appears to be antigen-driven, with several potential triggers under investigation.

The clonal expansion of both B cells and CD4+ T cells observed in patients strongly suggests that IgG4-RD is an antigen-driven condition (PMID: 38601138, 25865251). This process is thought to initiate a cascade involving T-helper 2 (Th2) and regulatory T cells, leading to IgG4 class switching, plasma cell proliferation, and eventual fibrosis (PMID: 27012661, 24107849). While the B cell response is central to the disease, as evidenced by the efficacy of rituximab, the specific antigens that trigger this complex immune response remain a primary focus of investigation (PMID: 25865251). Potential triggers are broadly categorized as autoantigens, infectious agents, and allergens (PMID: 32279291, 21045701).

2.3.1 Autoantigens such as annexin A11 and laminin 511-E8 have been identified in specific disease phenotypes.

The hypothesis that IgG4-RD is an autoimmune disease is supported by the identification of several candidate autoantigens (PMID: 31376893, 32279291). Although a single, unifying autoantigen has not been found, studies have identified antibodies against proteins such as annexin A11, laminin 511-E8, galectin-3, and prohibitin in subsets of patients (PMID: 34539675, 38601138). The prevalence of these autoantibodies varies; for instance, in one diverse cohort, antibodies to annexin A11 were found in 12% of patients, while antibodies to galectin-3 were present in 28% (PMID: 34539675). The relevance of some autoantigens appears to be phenotype-specific, with antibodies to laminin-511 detected in 50% of subjects with IgG4-related pancreatitis (PMID: 31376893). Further supporting its pathogenic role, immunization of mice with laminin-511 induced pancreatic injury, providing crucial experimental evidence (PMID: 31376893). However, the pathogenic significance of most identified autoantigens remains to be fully defined, and their discovery underscores the need for further research to correlate specific autoantibodies with organ involvement (PMID: 31376893, 38601138).

2.3.2 Infectious agents, including nontuberculous mycobacteria and other bacteria, may act as triggers in susceptible individuals.

Exogenous antigens from microorganisms have been investigated as potential initiators of the immune response in IgG4-RD (PMID: 30135634). For example, Helicobacter pylori has been explored as a possible trigger, though the role of microbes in inciting the disease remains largely speculative (PMID: 27514601). An alternative line of evidence comes from immunological parallels between IgG4-RD and certain infections. The immune-mediated response in IgG4-RD, characterized by the activation of helper T cells and the expression of specific cytokines, shares notable similarities with the host immune defense against tuberculosis (PMID: 36085248). This observation has led to the hypothesis that mycobacteria or other infectious agents could act as an initial trigger for the disease in genetically susceptible individuals, although direct causative links have not been established (PMID: 36085248, 25865251).

2.3.3 Allergens are associated with a subset of patients, suggesting a link between atopy and IgG4-RD pathogenesis.

The relationship between allergy and IgG4-RD is intriguing, as both conditions share key immunopathological features, including Th2 cell activation, elevated serum IgE, and eosinophilia (PMID: 32732179, 36798519). A significant proportion of patients with IgG4-RD report a history of atopic conditions like asthma, chronic sinusitis, or eczema (PMID: 36798519). This has fueled the hypothesis that allergic processes may contribute to or trigger IgG4-RD pathogenesis (PMID: 32732179, 21045701). However, the connection is complex and controversial. Eosinophilia and elevated IgE are also found in IgG4-RD patients without a history of atopy, suggesting that Th2 immune activation may be an intrinsic feature of the disease itself rather than being solely allergy-mediated (PMID: 36798519, 32732179). An alternative hypothesis posits that IgG4-RD could represent a late-stage manifestation of allergic disease, mirroring the immune profile seen after successful allergen immunotherapy (PMID: 32732179). Further studies are needed to clarify the precise mechanisms linking atopy and the development of IgG4-RD.

2.4 Genetic factors, including specific HLA haplotypes, contribute to disease susceptibility.

Genetic predisposition is a significant factor in the development of IgG4-RD, with studies identifying associations with both human leukocyte antigen (HLA) and non-HLA genes (PMID: 32732179, 27514601). Research using candidate-gene and genome-wide association study (GWAS) approaches has revealed that these genetic risk factors can vary among different ethnic populations (PMID: 33681509, 22151922). For instance, studies on type 1 autoimmune pancreatitis (AIP), a classic manifestation of IgG4-RD, have linked susceptibility to the HLA-DRB10405-DQB10401 haplotype in Japanese patients and DQβ1–57 in Korean patients (PMID: 32279291, 35545574). Furthermore, certain non-HLA genes related to immune regulation, such as cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4 (CTLA4), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and Fc receptor-like 3 (FCRL3), have also been implicated (PMID: 27514601, 32279291). The association of chronic periaortitis with HLA-DRB1*03, a risk factor for other classic autoimmune diseases, further strengthens the hypothesis of an autoimmune origin for IgG4-RD (PMID: 30397845). These findings collectively underscore the role of genes governing the immune response in conferring susceptibility to the disease (PMID: 27514601).

2.5 The development of fibrosis is a key pathological process leading to irreversible organ damage.

IgG4-RD is fundamentally a fibro-inflammatory disease, where the progressive development of fibrosis is a central pathological feature that leads to permanent organ damage and dysfunction (PMID: 27012661, 40528331, 30397845). Histological examination of affected tissues typically reveals a dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate alongside a characteristic, often extensive, pattern of fibrosis described as "storiform" (PMID: 30135634). This fibrotic process is thought to be driven by a self-antigen-triggered T-helper cell response (PMID: 25481618). Specific mechanisms may involve cytokines such as transforming growth factor-beta 1 (TGF-β1), which is known to activate fibroblasts and promote tissue fibrosis (PMID: 30135634). If left untreated, the relentless fibrotic remodeling can cause irreversible harm; for example, up to 60% of patients with pancreatic involvement present with endocrine or exocrine insufficiency due to end-stage fibrosis (PMID: 40528331). Although the fibrotic damage itself is not reversible, early diagnosis and treatment can control the active inflammation, reduce the size of lesions, and prevent further organ injury (PMID: 27012661, 38601138).

2.6 Animal models are being developed to recapitulate human disease and explore pathogenic mechanisms.

To dissect the complex pathogenesis of IgG4-RD and evaluate novel therapeutic strategies, researchers are actively developing animal models (PMID: 40628311, 21045701). These models are essential for understanding the interplay between genetic factors, environmental triggers, and the immune dysregulation that characterizes the disease (PMID: 33681509, 25481618). However, creating a model that fully recapitulates the human condition has proven challenging (PMID: 40628311). Significant hurdles include the unique biological properties of the human IgG4 molecule and key genetic differences between humans and commonly used laboratory animals, such as mice. Consequently, while several humanized and non-humanized models can replicate certain aspects of IgG4-RD, none yet encompass all the pathological hallmarks observed in patients (PMID: 40628311). Continued refinement of these experimental systems is critical for advancing knowledge of disease mechanisms and for the preclinical development of more effective treatments (PMID: 40628311, 24296677).

3 Clinical and Radiological Manifestations of IgG4-RRD

3.1 Clinical presentation of IgG4-RRD is often characterized by nonspecific respiratory symptoms or may be asymptomatic.

The clinical presentation of IgG4-related respiratory disease (IgG4-RRD) is often subtle and nonspecific, with a significant number of patients being entirely asymptomatic (PMID: 34911521, 26809651). In many cases, pulmonary abnormalities are discovered incidentally on chest imaging performed during workups for extra-thoracic disease or for unrelated reasons (PMID: 34911521, 30474465, 27514601). When symptoms are present, they are highly variable and depend on the anatomical location of the lesions (PMID: 34911521, 26809651). The most frequently reported respiratory symptoms include cough, dyspnea, chest or back pain, and hemoptysis (PMID: 34911521, 26809651, 38769126, 27514601). Systemic symptoms like low-grade fever and weight loss may also be present, with some studies suggesting that constitutional symptoms are not as rare as previously thought (PMID: 26809651, 27514601). Furthermore, a notable subset of patients (40-50%) has a history of allergic rhinitis or bronchial asthma, and may present with asthmatic symptoms like cough and wheezing (PMID: 30474465). This nonspecific and heterogeneous presentation means IgG4-RRD can mimic a wide array of more common conditions, including asthma, infections, sarcoidosis, and malignancies, posing a significant diagnostic challenge (PMID: 29992203, 38769126).

3.2 Thoracic involvement in IgG4-RD is heterogeneous and can affect any compartment of the chest.

3.2.1 Parenchymal lung disease manifests as several distinct radiological patterns.

3.2.1.1 Solid nodules or mass-like lesions (inflammatory pseudotumors) are a common finding that can mimic malignancy.

One of the most frequent radiological manifestations of parenchymal IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is the presence of solid nodules or mass-like lesions (PMID: 27514601, 32279291). These lesions often present as focal opacities, may have spiculated margins, and can be confined to a single lobe, closely mimicking a primary pulmonary malignancy (PMID: 27445515, 37018367). The diagnostic challenge is compounded by the fact that these lesions, along with associated hilar or mediastinal lymphadenopathy, frequently demonstrate intense 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) uptake on positron emission tomography (PET) scans, further heightening suspicion for cancer (PMID: 27445515, 27514601, 32279291). It is now understood that many cases previously diagnosed as inflammatory pseudotumors of the lung, particularly the plasma cell type, represent a manifestation of IgG4-RD (PMID: 27514601, 30474465, 32279291).

3.2.1.2 Interstitial patterns include organizing pneumonia, ground-glass opacities, and reticular changes.

Beyond focal masses, IgG4-RD can manifest as a diffuse interstitial lung disease (ILD) with varied radiological patterns (PMID: 27514601, 32279291). Common findings on computed tomography (CT) include ground-glass opacities (GGO), reticular opacities, and thickening of the bronchovascular bundles and interlobular septa, which are often located in the peripheral and subpleural lung (PMID: 27514601, 31376893, 36238052). Histopathologically, these radiological patterns can correspond to several forms of interstitial pneumonia, most commonly nonspecific interstitial pneumonia (NSIP), organizing pneumonia (OP), or lymphoid interstitial pneumonia (LIP) (PMID: 27514601, 32279291, 31376899). While advanced fibrotic changes such as honeycombing are uncommon, they have been reported (PMID: 31376893, 26481243). As these patterns are not specific to IgG4-RD, the diagnosis ultimately depends on histopathological analysis demonstrating a characteristic lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate with an abundance of IgG4-positive plasma cells (PMID: 27514601).

3.2.1.3 Alveolar consolidation with air bronchograms is another recognized presentation.

Alveolar consolidation is another recognized pattern of parenchymal involvement in IgG4-RD (PMID: 27514601, 31376893). This finding, which may contain characteristic air bronchograms, can appear as a dense, space-occupying infiltrate on imaging. It is considered one of the frequent intrapulmonary manifestations and is often observed in the lung periphery (PMID: 31376893). This presentation can occur in isolation or in conjunction with other radiological features of IgG4-RD, such as ground-glass opacities, nodules, and septal thickening (PMID: 31376893). The appearance can be categorized as an "alveolar interstitial type" and contributes to the broad spectrum of radiological findings that can make IgG4-RD difficult to distinguish from other pulmonary pathologies (PMID: 26481243).

3.2.2 Airway involvement typically presents as thickening of the bronchial or peribronchovascular walls.

Airway involvement is a primary feature of thoracic IgG4-RD, typically presenting on imaging as thickening of the bronchial or peribronchovascular walls (PMID: 34615698, 32732179). This finding is considered one of the most frequent patterns of thoracic disease and is recognized in the diagnostic classification criteria for IgG4-RD (PMID: 34615698, 36437514).

3.2.3 Pleural disease can manifest as pleural thickening or effusions, which may be exudative, lymphocytic, or chylous.

Pleural disease, while less common than airway involvement, is another important manifestation of thoracic IgG4-RD (PMID: 34615698). It can present as pleural thickening, masses, or effusions (PMID: 30474465, 31376893). These features may occur in isolation but are more frequently seen with concurrent parenchymal or systemic disease (PMID: 25422997, 27514601). Pleural effusions are characteristically exudative and lymphocytic, containing plasma cells, while biopsies reveal a lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate with abundant IgG4-positive cells and fibrosis (PMID: 30474465, 27514601).

3.2.4 Mediastinal involvement most frequently includes lymphadenopathy and, less commonly, fibrosing mediastinitis.

The mediastinum is a common site of involvement, with some studies reporting thoracic manifestations in over 40% of IgG4-RD patients (PMID: 29229083). Mediastinal lymphadenopathy is the most frequent finding and, along with peribronchovascular thickening, is considered a hallmark pattern of thoracic disease (PMID: 34615698, 29229083). Less frequently, mediastinal involvement can manifest as a distinct mass or as fibrosing mediastinitis (PMID: 32279291, 34615698).

3.2.5 Vascular structures such as the aorta and pulmonary arteries can be affected, leading to aortitis or pulmonary hypertension.

Vascular structures within the chest can also be affected by IgG4-RD (PMID: 36437514). A notable manifestation is IgG4-related aortitis, which can involve the thoracic aorta and its branches, potentially leading to complications such as aneurysms or dissections (PMID: 25481618).

3.3 Distinct clinical phenotypes have been proposed based on pathological features and clinical presentation.

3.3.1 A distinction between proliferative and fibrotic phenotypes may predict response to therapy.

The clinical and pathological spectrum of IgG4-related respiratory disease (IgG4-RLD) is characterized by a diverse range of fibroinflammatory manifestations. Recent evidence suggests that identifying distinct disease phenotypes is essential for tailoring targeted treatments and predicting clinical outcomes (PMID: 33689549). Pathologically, the disease is defined by a dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate, storiform fibrosis, and obliterative phlebitis, alongside an increased IgG4+/IgG+ plasma cell ratio (>40%) (PMID: 29992203, 36437514).

A critical distinction exists between the proliferative (inflammatory) and fibrotic phenotypes. The proliferative stage, marked by active lymphoplasmacytic infiltration and high IgG4+ cell counts, is generally more responsive to early intervention, which can reduce disease progression (PMID: 38769126). Conversely, as the disease progresses to a fibrotic stage, characterized by a "cartwheel" pattern of storiform fibrosis and the proliferation of spindle cells (fibroblasts and myofibroblasts), the histopathological evidence may become less specific, potentially leading to a reduced response to conventional immunosuppressive therapy (PMID: 35221040, 36437514). Furthermore, the lung often exhibits stereotypic morphologic responses to injury, making the interpretation of biopsies challenging due to overlap with other fibroinflammatory conditions (PMID: 23068301). While asymptomatic patients with stable lung nodules may only require monitoring, those with advanced pulmonary involvement face higher morbidity, necessitating phenotype-specific management strategies (PMID: 38769126, 33689549).

3.3.2 The presence of atopy and asthma is associated with specific clinical and serological features.

Atopy and asthma are frequently observed in patients with IgG4-related disease and are associated with distinct clinical and serological profiles. In particular, the presence of asthma has been strongly linked to specific manifestations such as orbital IgG4-RD and comorbid sinusitis (PMID: 26782673). Serologically, patients with asthma and IgG4-RD often exhibit significantly higher serum IgG4 values compared to those without respiratory airway involvement (PMID: 26782673).

Distinguishing asthma within the context of IgG4-RD is also vital for differential diagnosis, particularly to exclude other eosinophilic or vasculitic conditions like eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA), which shares features of reactive airway disease and eosinophilic infiltrates (PMID: 31376899, 29992203). The clinical diagnosis of asthma in these patients, often defined by physician assessment and the exclusion of heavy smoking histories, serves as a key indicator of a specific disease endotype that may require integrated management of both systemic fibroinflammation and airway hyperreactivity (PMID: 26782673, 37682160).

4 Diagnostic Approach

4.1 The diagnosis of IgG4-RRD requires a comprehensive clinicopathological correlation and a multidisciplinary approach.

The diagnosis of IgG4-related respiratory disease (IgG4-RRD) is not straightforward, as no single diagnostic test is available (PMID: 31376893, 38144610). Instead, establishing a diagnosis requires a comprehensive, multi-dimensional approach that integrates all available data from the history, physical examination, laboratory results, imaging, and pathology (PMID: 31376893, 40195520). This process involves the judicious correlation of clinical and radiological findings (PMID: 27514601, 38144610). However, serological and radiological features alone lack sufficient sensitivity and specificity for a definitive diagnosis (PMID: 25865251). Therefore, a definitive diagnosis of IgG4-RRD relies on meticulous clinicopathological correlation, with histopathological confirmation considered mandatory (PMID: 25865251). Histological examination serves the dual purpose of confirming features consistent with IgG4-RD and, critically, excluding neoplastic or other inflammatory conditions that can mimic the disease (PMID: 25865251, 40195520). Given the complexity of these cases and the broad spectrum of clinical presentations, a multidisciplinary team effort is essential to synthesize these diverse findings into an accurate diagnosis (PMID: 39644037, 37018367).

4.2 Serological and laboratory findings provide supportive evidence for the diagnosis.

4.2.1 Elevated serum IgG4 concentration is a hallmark feature but lacks sufficient sensitivity and specificity for a standalone diagnosis.

An elevated serum IgG4 concentration is a characteristic and hallmark finding in IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD) (PMID: 22596100, 28374231). A cutoff value of >135 mg/dL (1.35 g/L) is generally considered diagnostically significant (PMID: 29992203, 30135634). However, this marker has significant limitations in both sensitivity and specificity, making it insufficient for diagnosis when used in isolation (PMID: 28374231, 30135634). The sensitivity is compromised by the fact that a substantial portion of patients with biopsy-proven IgG4-RD, ranging from 20% to 45% in various studies, present with normal serum IgG4 levels (PMID: 22596100, 28374231, 29992203, 30135634). This is particularly relevant for patients with single-organ involvement, such as isolated hypertrophic pachymeningitis (PMID: 28374231). Conversely, the specificity of serum IgG4 is poor because elevated levels can be found in healthy individuals and in a wide array of other conditions that can mimic IgG4-RD, including parasitic infections, allergic diseases, other autoimmune disorders like rheumatoid arthritis and ANCA-associated vasculitis, multicentric Castleman's disease, and certain malignancies (PMID: 28374231, 29992203). While doubling the upper limit of the normal IgG4 concentration can increase specificity, it does so at the cost of unacceptably poor sensitivity (PMID: 25415530). Therefore, while an elevated serum IgG4 level provides supportive evidence, particularly when levels are very high or in cases of multi-organ disease, it is not a definitive diagnostic test (PMID: 28374231, 29992203). The diagnosis of IgG4-RD ultimately relies on the integration of characteristic clinical, radiological, and histopathological findings, including immunohistochemical staining for IgG4-positive plasma cells in tissue (PMID: 22596100, 38601138).

4.2.2 Other laboratory abnormalities may include polyclonal hypergammaglobulinemia, elevated IgE, eosinophilia, and hypocomplementemia.

Beyond serum IgG4, a panel of other laboratory abnormalities can provide supportive evidence for a diagnosis of IgG4-RD, although they are generally nonspecific. Polyclonal hypergammaglobulinemia is a common finding (PMID: 26481243, 32279291, 28284385). Reflecting the atopic diathesis often associated with the disease, many patients exhibit elevated serum IgE concentrations and mild peripheral eosinophilia, with the latter seen in approximately one-third of cases (PMID: 26481243, 32279291, 38601138, 40528331). Another key finding is hypocomplementemia, characterized by low levels of C3 and C4, which is frequently observed in patients with IgG4-related kidney disease but can also be present in those without overt renal involvement (PMID: 26481243, 28284385, 31376893). The pattern of low C3 and very low or undetectable C4 can resemble that seen in systemic lupus erythematosus or mixed cryoglobulinemia (PMID: 31376893). Other nonspecific findings may include elevated inflammatory markers such as the erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP), anemia, and the presence of low-titer autoantibodies like antinuclear antibodies (ANA) and rheumatoid factor (RF) (PMID: 26481243, 32279291). These markers, particularly serum IgG4, IgE, complement levels, and eosinophil counts, can also be valuable for monitoring disease activity and predicting relapses (PMID: 38601138).

4.3 Thoracic imaging is crucial for identifying patterns of involvement and guiding further investigation.

4.3.1 High-resolution computed tomography (CT) is the primary imaging modality for characterizing thoracic lesions.

Chest computed tomography (CT) is the cornerstone imaging modality for evaluating thoracic involvement in IgG4-RD, offering significantly greater sensitivity than conventional radiography (PMID: 36437514, 31376893). For optimal characterization of lesions, high-resolution CT (HRCT) with thin sections is the ideal approach (PMID: 36437514). The manifestations of thoracic IgG4-RD are polymorphic and often non-specific, capable of affecting any structure in the chest, including the lungs, airways, mediastinum, and pleura (PMID: 36437514, 31376893). Common CT patterns include peribronchovascular thickening, thoracic lymphadenopathy, solid nodules, ground-glass opacities, and pleural thickening (PMID: 34615698, 29992203). Given that some findings can be subtle, review by radiologists with expertise in thoracic imaging and familiarity with IgG4-RD is recommended to improve diagnostic accuracy (PMID: 36437514, 34615698). In the absence of a biopsy, the presence of multiple characteristic thoracic patterns can increase diagnostic confidence, and classification criteria have been proposed to help determine the likelihood that observed lesions are attributable to IgG4-RD (PMID: 36437514).

4.3.2 18F-FDG positron emission tomography/computed tomography (PET/CT) is useful for assessing disease extent, activity, and guiding biopsy.

18F-fluorodeoxyglucose positron emission tomography/computed tomography (18F-FDG PET/CT) is a valuable functional imaging tool for the systemic evaluation of IgG4-RD (PMID: 32732179). By highlighting hypermetabolic activity, PET/CT can map sites of active inflammation throughout the body, making it highly effective for establishing the full extent of the disease at initial diagnosis (PMID: 26481243, 32732179). It is particularly useful for detecting clinically asymptomatic organ involvement and may have greater sensitivity than conventional imaging for sites such as arteries, salivary glands, and lymph nodes (PMID: 36437514, 26259069). Clinically, PET/CT plays a crucial role in guiding biopsies to the most metabolically active and diagnostically informative sites, assessing disease activity, monitoring response to therapy, and detecting relapse (PMID: 32279291, 26481243). However, the increased FDG uptake is not specific to IgG4-RD and can be seen in other inflammatory conditions and malignancies, meaning histopathological confirmation is often required to differentiate these conditions (PMID: 26481243, 32687790).

4.3.3 Emerging molecular imaging techniques, such as fibroblast activation protein inhibitor (FAPI) PET, show promise in visualizing fibrotic activity.

Emerging molecular imaging techniques are being explored to overcome the limitations of conventional imaging, particularly for the fibrotic phenotype of IgG4-RD. Radiolabeled fibroblast activation protein inhibitors (FAPIs) are promising tracers that target and visualize fibroblast activation, a key pathological process in fibrosis and tissue remodeling (PMID: 40753024). FAPI-PET shows potential for staging and monitoring fibrotic IgG4-RD, a setting where traditional inflammatory biomarkers may be normal and FDG-PET can be less informative (PMID: 39644037). Initial studies have shown that FAPI tracer uptake correlates well with the presence of activated fibroblasts in affected tissues (PMID: 39644037). IgG4-RD is a recognized non-oncological application for FAPI-PET research, which is expanding rapidly (PMID: 40753024, 36437424). However, FAPI-PET remains an investigational modality that is not yet widely available for clinical use and requires further validation in prospective studies to establish its role in the management of IgG4-RD (PMID: 39644037, 36437424).

4.4 Histopathological examination of tissue biopsies is the cornerstone of a definitive diagnosis.

Histopathological analysis of an affected organ is the gold standard for diagnosing IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD), providing essential evidence to distinguish it from mimics like malignancy, infection, or other inflammatory conditions (PMID: 25481618, 30135634). A definitive diagnosis relies on the integration of characteristic morphological findings with immunohistochemical staining, all interpreted within the appropriate clinical context through close collaboration between clinicians and pathologists (PMID: 32732179, 22596100).

4.4.1 The characteristic histological triad consists of a dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate, storiform fibrosis, and obliterative phlebitis.

The classic histopathological hallmarks of IgG4-RD consist of a triad of features: (1) a dense, polyclonal lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate; (2) a distinctive storiform fibrosis; and (3) obliterative phlebitis (PMID: 36437514, 26481243, 28374231). The storiform fibrosis is characterized by an irregularly whorled or cartwheel pattern of fibroblasts and connective tissue (PMID: 26481243, 27445515). Obliterative phlebitis, the inflammation and subsequent occlusion of veins, is considered a highly specific finding (PMID: 28374231). A mild-to-moderate eosinophilic infiltrate is also frequently observed (PMID: 28374231). While this constellation of features is highly suggestive of IgG4-RD, it is important to note that not all three may be present in a single biopsy specimen (PMID: 32732179). In some organs, including the lung, storiform fibrosis or obliterative phlebitis may be inconspicuous or absent, and a confident diagnosis may be made with just two of the major features, typically the dense infiltrate and fibrosis (PMID: 22596100, 28374231).

4.4.2 Immunohistochemistry demonstrating an increased number of IgG4-positive plasma cells and an elevated IgG4/IgG ratio is required.

In addition to the characteristic morphology, the diagnosis of IgG4-RD requires immunohistochemical evidence of an increased number of IgG4-positive plasma cells in the tissue infiltrate (PMID: 22596100). Although consensus is still evolving, commonly accepted criteria for a positive finding include an absolute count of >50 IgG4-positive plasma cells per high-power field and/or a ratio of IgG4-positive to IgG-positive plasma cells greater than 40% (PMID: 40528331, 33978820, 39644037). However, these findings alone are not specific to IgG4-RD, as elevated IgG4-positive cells can be seen in other inflammatory conditions and around neoplasms (PMID: 22596100, 30135634). Therefore, the diagnosis cannot be established solely on immunohistochemistry; it must be supported by the characteristic histopathological appearance (PMID: 22596100, 39644037). The combination of morphological features and immunohistochemical staining, interpreted with sound clinical judgment, forms the basis for a definitive diagnosis (PMID: 22596100).

4.4.3 Obtaining adequate tissue via methods like surgical biopsy or cryo-transbronchial lung biopsy is essential for accurate assessment.

To enable a comprehensive histopathological assessment, obtaining a sufficiently large tissue sample is critical (PMID: 38144610). Fine-needle aspiration is generally inadequate because it provides only cells, precluding evaluation of essential architectural features like storiform fibrosis and obliterative phlebitis (PMID: 30135634). In the context of respiratory disease, less invasive methods such as CT-guided core-needle biopsy may fail to yield a definitive diagnosis in a significant number of cases (PMID: 26809651, 38144610). Consequently, surgical lung biopsy via video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS) or thoracotomy has often been necessary to acquire adequate tissue for a confident diagnosis (PMID: 26481243, 26809651). More recently, transbronchial lung cryobiopsy has emerged as a promising, less invasive alternative. This technique can retrieve larger and better-preserved specimens than traditional forceps biopsies, potentially avoiding the need for surgery in some patients (PMID: 38144610, 26809651).

4.5 Standardized diagnostic and classification criteria have been established to improve diagnostic consistency.

4.5.1 The 2019 ACR/EULAR classification criteria provide a structured approach for classifying patients for research.

In 2019, the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) and the European League Against Rheumatism (EULAR) jointly published new classification criteria for IgG4-RD, representing a significant advance in standardizing the disease definition for research purposes (PMID: 36437514, 36238052). Developed by a large international, multispecialty group that reviewed over 1,000 patient cases, these criteria were designed to create homogenous patient cohorts for clinical studies (PMID: 40528331, 33689549). The process follows a three-step approach: 1) an entry criterion requiring characteristic clinical, radiological, or pathological involvement in at least one of 11 specified organs; 2) a comprehensive set of 32 exclusion criteria to rule out mimics such as malignancy, infection, and other immune-mediated diseases (e.g., those with specific autoantibodies like ANCA or anti-SSA/Ro); and 3) a weighted, points-based system of eight inclusion domains covering clinical, serological, radiological, and histological findings. A score of 20 or higher is required to classify a patient as having IgG4-RD (PMID: 36238052, 40528331). While these criteria were developed to maximize specificity for the most commonly involved organs, their primary aim is to provide an invaluable and rigorous platform for designing high-quality research and clinical trials, rather than serving as a direct diagnostic tool for individual patient management (PMID: 40528331, 33689549, 38769126). Their implementation has been crucial for improving the quality and comparability of studies on IgG4-RD (PMID: 37561593, 38769126).

4.5.2 The 2022 revised diagnostic criteria for IgG4-RRD offer specific guidance for respiratory involvement.

While the ACR/EULAR criteria serve research, specific diagnostic guidance is crucial for clinical practice, particularly for organ-specific manifestations like IgG4-related respiratory disease (IgG4-RRD). The first comprehensive diagnostic criteria for IgG4-RD were proposed in Japan in 2011, followed by organ-specific criteria for IgG4-RRD in 2015 (PMID: 37714094, 31104539). More recently, updated diagnostic criteria for IgG4-RRD were presented, building upon revisions to the comprehensive criteria and the publication of the ACR/EULAR classification criteria (PMID: 37714094). These revised guidelines emphasize a meticulous diagnostic process, placing a strong focus on the evaluation of specific pathological findings and the rigorous exclusion of other respiratory diseases that can mimic IgG4-RRD (PMID: 37714094). This is particularly important given that definitive diagnosis can be challenging due to ambiguous presentations and difficulties in obtaining adequate tissue samples, potentially leading to treatment delays (PMID: 38769126, 30474465). The criteria specifically advise clinicians to reconsider the diagnosis and explore alternative conditions in cases where imaging reveals chronic fibrosis or where there is a poor response to steroid therapy (PMID: 37714094). This structured approach aims to improve diagnostic accuracy beyond earlier methods that often relied heavily on serum IgG4 levels, a marker now understood to have limited specificity (PMID: 25422997).

5 Differential Diagnosis

5.1 IgG4-RRD must be distinguished from various malignancies that it can mimic clinically and radiologically.

5.1.1 Differentiating from lung cancer is a primary challenge, especially in cases presenting as solitary nodules or masses.

IgG4-related lung disease (IgG4-RLD) is a rare systemic condition characterized by diverse and overlapping radiological features that frequently mimic primary lung tumors (PMID: 36276215, 37018367). Distinguishing IgG4-RLD from lung cancer represents a significant clinical challenge, particularly when the disease manifests as a solitary solid nodule or mass (PMID: 34911521, 36276215). The incidence of lung nodules in patients with IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is notably high, and while a nodule in a patient with known systemic IgG4-RD is likely related to the primary disease, it remains difficult to differentiate from malignancy based on imaging alone (PMID: 32795329). Specific radiologic patterns, such as the bronchovascular type, may mimic lymphangitic carcinomatosis, while pleural involvement presenting as nodules or effusions can be mistaken for disseminated metastatic disease (PMID: 36276215).

Diagnostic support for IgG4-RLD over malignancy includes the presence of multiple imaging findings, elevated serum IgG4 levels, involvement of other organs, and the absence of significant increases in serum tumor biomarkers (PMID: 34911521, 36276215). Furthermore, a positive clinical response to experimental glucocorticoid or immunosuppressive therapy can aid in the differential diagnosis (PMID: 32795329, 34911521). However, because these clinical markers are not always present or definitive, surgical or lung biopsy is often required to establish a definitive diagnosis and rule out malignancy (PMID: 36276215, 34911521, 37018367).

5.1.2 Lymphoproliferative disorders, such as lymphoma and Castleman disease, are important considerations in the differential.

Beyond solid tumors, IgG4-RLD must be differentiated from various lymphoproliferative and inflammatory disorders, most notably lymphoma and multicentric Castleman disease (MCD) (PMID: 32732179, 30883447, 25481618). This differentiation is complicated by the fact that both lymphoma and Castleman disease can present with elevated serum IgG4 levels and an increased number of IgG4+ plasma cells in tissue (PMID: 32732179, 26259069, 31227290). Other conditions that may mimic the clinical or histological features of IgG4-RLD include Rosai-Dorfman disease, sarcoidosis, and various connective tissue diseases (PMID: 30883447, 31227290, 25481618).

Histopathological examination remains the gold standard for distinguishing these entities. While IgG4-RLD is characterized by a lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate, it is specifically identified by the presence of storiform fibrosis, obliterative phlebitis, and eosinophilic infiltrates (PMID: 26259069, 31227290). In contrast, MCD typically lacks these specific fibrotic features and exhibits lesions in the alveoli adjacent to the perilymphatic stromal area, rather than within the stroma itself (PMID: 31227290). Rosai-Dorfman disease can be distinguished by the presence of S-100 protein-positive histiocytes demonstrating emperipolesis (PMID: 31227290). Given the potential for misdiagnosis and the necessity of early, accurate diagnosis to prevent irreversible organ damage and fibrosis, a comprehensive evaluation of histopathology, serology, and clinical presentation is essential (PMID: 30135634, 26259069).

5.2 Exclusion of infectious diseases is critical before initiating immunosuppressive therapy.

The diagnosis of IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is fundamentally a diagnosis of exclusion, requiring the careful elimination of other conditions that present with similar clinical, radiological, and/or histopathological features (PMID: 27514601, 28070877). The differential diagnosis is broad and includes various inflammatory, neoplastic, and infectious conditions (PMID: 25865251, 30135634). Among these, the exclusion of infectious diseases is of paramount importance, particularly before treatment is initiated. The standard first-line therapy for IgG4-RD involves immunosuppressive agents, most commonly corticosteroids (PMID: 30295799). Administering such treatment to a patient with an undiagnosed infection can lead to severe adverse outcomes. Therefore, a thorough evaluation to rule out infections that can mimic IgG4-RD, such as syphilis or HIV, is a critical step in the diagnostic pathway (PMID: 26481243). A definitive diagnosis must be established through a comprehensive approach that correlates clinical and radiographic data with histopathological and immunohistochemical findings to ensure patient safety and appropriate management (PMID: 25422997, 25865251).

5.3 There is significant overlap with other systemic inflammatory and autoimmune diseases affecting the lungs.

5.3.1 Sarcoidosis, ANCA-associated vasculitis, and Sjögren's syndrome can present with similar thoracic manifestations.

The differential diagnosis of IgG4-related respiratory disease (IgG4-RRD) is complicated by its significant clinical and radiological overlap with other inflammatory and autoimmune disorders. The thoracic manifestations of IgG4-RRD can closely resemble those of other conditions, particularly sarcoidosis and antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)-associated vasculitis (AAV) (PMID: 31376893). AAVs, which include granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA), and microscopic polyangiitis (MPA), are among the vasculitides that most frequently affect the lungs, and a known association exists between IgG4-RD and AAV (PMID: 31376899, 28070877). Similarly, connective tissue diseases can present with airway inflammation and fibrosis; Sjögren's syndrome is most frequently associated with these thoracic findings, creating another potential diagnostic mimic (PMID: 31376899, 37809072). The challenge is further compounded by the fact that some of these mimicking diseases can also be associated with elevated serum IgG4 levels, underscoring the need for a comprehensive diagnostic approach (PMID: 36437514).

5.3.2 Rare disorders like Erdheim-Chester disease and hyper-IL-6 syndrome can mimic IgG4-RD.

IgG4-RD is itself a rare, systemic fibroinflammatory condition that can be considered a “great mimicker” due to its varied clinical and radiological presentations (PMID: 36276215, 37632955). Its pulmonary manifestations are highly variable, including infiltrates, nodules, mediastinal lymphadenopathy, and pleural disease, which may present with non-specific symptoms like cough, dyspnoea, or chest pain (PMID: 38769126). Consequently, the differential diagnosis for IgG4-RRD is exceptionally broad, encompassing a wide spectrum of conditions ranging from common to rare. It can mimic malignancies such as lung cancer and lymphoma, infections like pneumonia, and other immune-mediated conditions (PMID: 38769126, 40615976). The differentiation from lung cancer is particularly challenging, as IgG4-RRD can present as a solid nodule or mass, with other features mimicking lymphangitic carcinomatosis or metastasis (PMID: 36276215, 37018367). It can even be mistaken for more common respiratory conditions like refractory asthma (PMID: 29992203). This extensive mimicry necessitates that IgG4-RRD be considered in the differential diagnosis of unexplained inflammatory processes, though definitive diagnosis often requires histopathological confirmation (PMID: 29992203, 36276215).

6 Treatment and Management

6.1 Glucocorticoids are the established first-line therapy for inducing remission in active disease.

Glucocorticoids (GCs) are the cornerstone of first-line therapy for inducing remission in active IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD), a strategy supported by international consensus (PMID: 33689549, 27445515). Treatment is indicated for all symptomatic patients and a subset of asymptomatic individuals, with the goal of resolving symptoms and normalizing biochemical and radiological findings (PMID: 33689549, 27445515). For remission induction, a common regimen is oral prednisolone at a dose of 30–40 mg daily, while pulsed intravenous methylprednisolone may be an option for severe, multisystem disease (PMID: 29992203). A rapid and favorable response to GCs is a characteristic feature of IgG4-RD; indeed, a lack of responsiveness should prompt a re-evaluation of the diagnosis and is considered an exclusion criterion in the ACR/EULAR classification criteria (PMID: 37792260, 29992203). While highly effective for induction, the long-term toxicity associated with GCs necessitates the use of steroid-sparing agents for maintaining remission (PMID: 33689549, 27445515). In some cases, particularly those with organ-threatening disease, B-cell depletion therapy may be considered as part of the initial induction strategy alongside GCs (PMID: 27445515, 26481243).

6.2 Steroid-sparing agents are frequently required for maintaining remission and managing relapses.

While glucocorticoids are the first-line treatment for inducing remission in IgG4-RD, their long-term use is associated with significant toxicity, and a substantial number of patients experience disease relapse upon dose reduction or cessation (PMID: 29229083, 25415530). Consequently, steroid-sparing agents are frequently necessary for maintaining remission, managing relapses, or in patients with contraindications or unacceptable side effects from corticosteroids (PMID: 29229083, 27445515, 40809241).

6.2.1 Conventional immunosuppressants such as azathioprine and mycophenolate mofetil are used as maintenance therapy.

Conventional immunosuppressants are often employed as second-line or maintenance therapy in IgG4-RD. Agents such as mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) and azathioprine (AZA) are commonly used as steroid-sparing strategies to facilitate corticosteroid tapering, maintain remission, or manage relapses (PMID: 26481243, 26259069, 28070877). These drugs may be considered when glucocorticoid-related toxicity is a concern, or when the steroid dose cannot be lowered due to persistent disease activity (PMID: 28070877, 27514601). Other conventional agents, including methotrexate and cyclophosphamide, have also been utilized (PMID: 29229083, 26259069). However, the evidence supporting their use is derived primarily from observational studies and case series, with a lack of prospective data to guide agent selection or dosing strategies (PMID: 29229083, 27514601). Clinical practice is therefore highly variable and often based on physician experience (PMID: 29229083). The efficacy of these agents for maintaining remission is considered modest, with reported response rates for AZA and MMF around 40-50% (PMID: 39644037). It has been suggested that their primary function may be steroid-sparing rather than providing a synergistic effect with glucocorticoids (PMID: 28070877).

6.2.2 B-cell depletion therapy with rituximab has demonstrated high efficacy in relapsed or refractory cases.

B-cell depletion therapy with the anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody, rituximab, has emerged as a highly effective treatment for IgG4-RD, particularly in patients with relapsed or refractory disease (PMID: 39644037, 26259069). Rituximab acts by depleting the pool of CD20+ B-cells, which are precursors to the short-lived plasmablasts and plasma cells that produce IgG4, thereby leading to a rapid decline in serum IgG4 levels (PMID: 31227290, 29229083). Clinical studies have demonstrated its efficacy as a rescue therapy, even in patients who failed to achieve durable remission with conventional steroid-sparing agents (PMID: 27514601). An open-label trial reported favorable disease responses in 97% of participants treated with rituximab, many of whom had previously relapsed after treatment with corticosteroids and other immunosuppressants (PMID: 27514601, 37792260). While rituximab is most commonly used for rescue, it is also utilized for remission induction (PMID: 29229083, 26481243). Despite its high efficacy, challenges remain; evidence is largely limited to open-label studies, and there is no consensus on the optimal timing or frequency of treatment (PMID: 37792260, 29229083). Furthermore, while initial response rates are high, many patients require retreatment to maintain remission, and complete remission at 12 months was achieved in only 40% of patients in one study, highlighting an unmet need for improved maintenance strategies (PMID: 39644037, 37792260).

6.3 Novel therapies targeting specific pathogenic pathways are emerging as promising treatment options.

6.3.1 Targeting B-cell and plasma cell lineages with agents like inebilizumab (anti-CD19) is under investigation in clinical trials.

The rationale for targeting the B-cell lineage is strong, as clonal expansions of CD19+ plasmablasts correlate with disease activity and are associated with relapse after anti-CD20 therapy (PMID: 37792260). Inebilizumab, a humanized anti-CD19 monoclonal antibody, depletes a broader range of B-cells than rituximab, including plasmablasts, without activating complement (PMID: 37792260). A phase 2 trial showed positive outcomes (PMID: 36437514), and more definitively, the phase 3 MITIGATE trial demonstrated that inebilizumab significantly reduced the risk of disease relapse and increased the likelihood of flare-free remission at one year compared to placebo (PMID: 40342085). These findings confirm that CD19-targeted B-cell depletion is a potential future strategy for maintenance therapy in IgG4-RD (PMID: 40342085). Other agents targeting this pathway, including obexelimab (targeting the CD19-FcγRIIb receptor), belimumab (anti-BAFF), daratumumab (anti-CD38), and bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor), are also under investigation (PMID: 40342085, 36437514, 39644037).

6.3.2 CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy has shown potential for inducing durable, treatment-free remission in refractory disease.

For patients with severe, treatment-refractory IgG4-RD, CD19-directed chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy is emerging as a transformative approach (PMID: 41371348). In a reported case of a patient with multiorgan disease refractory to long-term immunosuppression, a single infusion of autologous CD19-directed CAR T-cells was well-tolerated and induced a deep and durable remission (PMID: 41371348). The treatment led to B-cell aplasia, normalization of serum IgG4 levels, regression of fibroinflammatory activity on imaging, and improved lung function. Crucially, the patient remained in a treatment-free remission for over 12 months, with immune profiling suggesting that CAR T-cell therapy can reset the B-cell compartment and dampen pathogenic T-cell and stromal interactions. This case provides proof-of-concept that CAR T-cell therapy can induce durable, drug-free remission and supports its prospective evaluation in refractory IgG4-RD (PMID: 41371348).

6.3.3 Other targeted therapies against T-cells, cytokines, and fibrotic pathways are being explored.

Beyond B-cell-directed treatments, research is expanding to other pathogenic axes in IgG4-RD (PMID: 33689549, 32279291). Therapies targeting T-cells, such as the T-cell co-stimulation inhibitor abatacept, are being studied (PMID: 38601138, 33689549). While targeting key pathogenic cytokines represents an attractive therapeutic strategy, clinical evidence remains limited to case reports, and controlled trials are currently lacking (PMID: 33689549). A significant unmet need is the development of effective anti-fibrotic treatments for advanced disease, which is a major cause of irreversible organ damage (PMID: 26481243). Addressing established fibrosis is a key priority for future trials and will likely require targeting multiple growth factor and cytokine pathways (PMID: 26481243, 33689549). These diverse investigational approaches reflect a deeper understanding of the disease's complex pathophysiology and the ongoing effort to develop more specific and effective alternatives to glucocorticoids (PMID: 32279291).

6.4 Management strategies should be tailored based on disease activity, organ involvement, and relapse patterns.

A personalized approach is crucial in the management of IgG4-RD, with the primary goals being the reduction of disease activity and the prevention of irreversible organ damage (PMID: 38601138). Treatment decisions should be tailored to individual patient characteristics, the specific organs involved, the degree of fibrosis, and relapse history (PMID: 38601138). For instance, in asymptomatic patients with limited disease, such as isolated lymph node or salivary gland involvement, a "watch-and-wait" strategy with close monitoring may be appropriate (PMID: 26481243, 38601138). Close and continuous monitoring is essential for all patients to confirm remission and detect relapses promptly (PMID: 38601138). This is achieved through a combination of clinical evaluation, laboratory tests like serum IgG4 levels, and organ-specific imaging (PMID: 32279291, 28374231, 26481243). Tools such as the IgG4-RD Responder Index have been developed to help standardize the assessment of disease activity and treatment response in clinical practice (PMID: 32279291, 26481243). This tailored strategy extends to managing relapses; for example, a purely serological relapse (rising IgG4) may warrant pre-emptive treatment in a patient with prior involvement of a high-risk organ, whereas in other cases, closer observation may suffice (PMID: 38601138).

7 Prognosis, Complications, and Special Considerations

7.1 The long-term prognosis is generally favorable with treatment, but irreversible organ damage from fibrosis can occur.

The primary goals of treating IgG4-RD are to induce and maintain remission, reduce disease activity, and prevent irreversible organ damage (PMID: 38601138). With appropriate therapy, most patients experience a significant reduction in lesion size and improvement in laboratory parameters, leading to a generally favorable long-term prognosis (PMID: 38601138). However, a major determinant of treatment response and ultimate outcome is the degree of fibrosis present at diagnosis (PMID: 38601138). The disease can progress from a proliferative, inflammatory stage to a more fibrotic phenotype, which is less responsive to immunosuppressive therapies (PMID: 39644037, 38601138). If left untreated or diagnosed late, progressive fibrosis can lead to permanent organ dysfunction, such as hydronephrosis from retroperitoneal fibrosis or respiratory failure from pulmonary lesions, and even death (PMID: 28091932, 37792260, 30135634). This underscores the critical importance of early diagnosis and intervention before extensive, irreversible fibrotic damage is established (PMID: 30135634).

7.2 Disease relapse is common, often occurring during or after glucocorticoid tapering.

While initial treatment with glucocorticoids is highly effective at inducing remission, a significant clinical challenge in managing IgG4-RD is the high rate of disease relapse (PMID: 28374231, 33689549). Relapses are common, frequently occurring as the glucocorticoid dose is tapered or after therapy is discontinued (PMID: 28374231, 25415530, 32279291). One systematic review reported a relapse risk of 33% in patients treated with first-line glucocorticoid regimens (PMID: 28374231). These relapses, which can lead to severe outcomes such as respiratory failure in patients with lung involvement, are typically responsive to re-treatment with glucocorticoids (PMID: 26809651, 32279291). However, repeated courses of glucocorticoids increase the risk of significant toxicity, particularly in the older demographic typical of IgG4-RD (PMID: 31376893). Consequently, strategies to mitigate relapse risk, such as long-term low-dose maintenance glucocorticoid therapy or the addition of steroid-sparing immunosuppressants, are often employed for long-term disease control (PMID: 32279291, 32732179).

7.3 An increased risk of malignancy, particularly lymphoma, has been reported in patients with IgG4-RD.

The relationship between IgG4-RD and malignancy remains a topic of considerable debate (PMID: 34986892, 25415530). Several observational studies and a meta-analysis have suggested that patients with IgG4-RD have an increased risk of developing cancer compared to the general population, with some reports indicating a risk several times higher (PMID: 34986892, 24107849, 21894525). Pancreatic cancer and lymphoma are among the malignancies most frequently reported in this patient group (PMID: 34986892). The association has led to speculation that IgG4-RD may represent a paraneoplastic or premalignant state in a subset of patients (PMID: 32279291). However, other studies have not found a significantly increased risk, and the current evidence is limited by its observational nature and relatively short follow-up periods (PMID: 34986892, 25415530). Given the unresolved nature of this association, careful long-term surveillance for malignancy is considered an important aspect of managing patients with IgG4-RD (PMID: 24107849, 34986892).

7.4 Hypocomplementemia is a marker of higher disease activity, multiorgan involvement, and increased relapse risk.

Hypocomplementemia, found in approximately 20–40% of individuals with IgG4-RD, has emerged as a key prognostic biomarker (PMID: 39079893). Its presence is strongly associated with a more severe disease phenotype, characterized by higher disease activity, a greater number of organs involved, and systemic involvement (PMID: 39079893). Importantly, patients with hypocomplementemia have a significantly higher risk of disease relapse (PMID: 35296071, 39079893). The mechanism appears to involve complement activation, with recent evidence suggesting that serum IgG1 concentration is independently associated with hypocomplementemia, pointing to an IgG1-mediated pathway (PMID: 39079893). Therefore, assessing complement levels can help identify patients with a more aggressive disease course who may require more intensive monitoring and potentially different therapeutic strategies (PMID: 39079893).

7.5 IgG4-RD in pediatric populations is rare and presents with unique clinical characteristics.

IgG4-RD is exceptionally rare in children, and its infrequency often contributes to significant delays in diagnosis (PMID: 33978820, 27012661). Although the condition is increasingly recognized in the pediatric population, the current understanding is based on a small number of published cases and retrospective series (PMID: 27012661, 33978820). The clinical presentation in children can differ from that in adults; while it can affect a wide range of organs, orbital manifestations are a particularly common feature in pediatric patients (PMID: 33978820). The limited data makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about epidemiology and management in this age group (PMID: 33978820). Increased awareness among pediatricians is crucial for early recognition, and further multicenter studies are needed to better characterize the disease and optimize treatment for children (PMID: 33978820, 27012661).

8 Conclusion and Future Directions

8.1 Recent years have seen substantial progress in understanding the pathophysiology, diagnosis, and treatment of IgG4-RRD.

In recent years, our knowledge of IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD), including its respiratory manifestations, has expanded substantially (PMID: 25415530, 32279291). This progress has led to increased clinical awareness and a shift towards improving the recognition and diagnosis of the condition (PMID: 25415530, 40243015). Important observations have been made regarding the contributions of different cell types and inflammatory pathways to the disease's pathophysiology (PMID: 25415530). This advancing understanding of pathogenic mechanisms is now paving the way for emerging therapeutic options that may serve as alternatives to glucocorticoids, the current standard of care (PMID: 33689549, 32279291). While many aspects of the disease still require elucidation, a firm foundation of knowledge has been established, providing a platform for future investigation and clinical refinement (PMID: 28070877).

8.2 Unanswered questions remain regarding disease triggers, the specific role of IgG4, and optimal long-term management.

Despite significant advances, fundamental questions about the pathogenesis of IgG4-RD persist. The etiology of the disease remains unclear, though genetic susceptibility factors have been suggested (PMID: 24296677). The initial triggers, whether microbial or self-antigens, have not been identified (PMID: 25865251). A central enigma is the precise role of the IgG4 antibody itself; it is not believed to be the primary pathogenic driver and its serum levels are not consistently elevated in all patients, complicating its use as a standalone diagnostic marker (PMID: 40528331, 22487688, 30295799). These major gaps in understanding the roles of T cells, B cells, and the innate immune system have hindered the development of specific diagnostic tests and animal models (PMID: 40528331, 25865251). Consequently, no definitive, universally accepted treatment strategy for long-term management has been established, with significant variability in practice even among specialists (PMID: 26481243). Managing the predominantly fibrotic, treatment-refractory aspects of the disease remains a major clinical challenge (PMID: 40528331).

8.3 Future research should focus on developing non-invasive biomarkers, conducting randomized controlled trials for new therapies, and establishing personalized treatment strategies.

Addressing the current knowledge gaps requires a multi-pronged research effort. A key priority is the development and validation of non-invasive biomarkers to facilitate accurate and rapid diagnosis, distinguish IgG4-RD from its mimics, and effectively monitor disease activity and treatment response (PMID: 25415530, 40342085). To improve therapeutic outcomes, there is a critical need for well-designed randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to rigorously assess the safety and efficacy of novel, targeted therapies (PMID: 33689549, 25415530). These trials should incorporate exploratory endpoints to provide deeper insights into drug mechanisms, disease processes, and patient-reported outcomes (PMID: 37792260). Ultimately, the goal is to move toward a more personalized medicine approach. This involves establishing tailored therapeutic strategies based on individual patient characteristics, organ-specific manifestations, disease stage, and the balance between inflammation and fibrosis (PMID: 38601138, 33689549, 38769126). Such personalized strategies are essential for inducing and maintaining remission, preventing irreversible organ damage, and optimizing long-term outcomes for patients with IgG4-RRD.
